What Is Relative Pitch

Relative pitch is the ability to identify a pitch, or note, when given a reference pitch first - it is different to perfect or ‘absolute’ pitch where an individual can identify a note without the need to hear a reference pitch. Perfect pitch is generally considered genetic or learned only at a very young age, whereas relative picth is a skill that can be developed and improved in almost everyone at any age.

Relative pitch is what forms the foundation of a good ‘musical ear’. If a musician has good relative pitch skills they could listen to a piece of music and figure out the melody notes, or the chord sequence, all in their head.

This App, and the Major Scale

This app focusses mostly on learning the sound, or ‘tonality’, of the Major scale. This is one of the most fundamentally useful musical skills in modern, particularly Western, music, where a significant majority of composition uses the Major scale as its foundational sonic palette - ooo what fancy words! This allows musicians with a solid internalisation of the major scale, and knowledge of basic music theory, the ability to effectively understand and navigate what a piece of music is doing, and move that onto their chosen instruments.

Identifying Musical Intervals

You'll hear two single notes in sequence. The second note will be higher than the first (except for Perfect Unison, where it is the same). Your task is to identify the interval (the distance in semitones) between them.

Intervals used in this game

  • Perfect Unison (P1) — 0 semitones
  • Minor Second (m2) — 1 semitone
  • Major Second (M2) — 2 semitones
  • Minor Third (m3) — 3 semitones
  • Major Third (M3) — 4 semitones
  • Perfect Fourth (P4) — 5 semitones
  • Augmented Fourth / Diminished Fifth (Tritone) (A4/d5) — 6 semitones
  • Perfect Fifth (P5) — 7 semitones
  • Minor Sixth (m6) — 8 semitones
  • Major Sixth (M6) — 9 semitones
  • Minor Seventh (m7) — 10 semitones
  • Major Seventh (M7) — 11 semitones
  • Perfect Octave (P8) — 12 semitones

Identifying Dominant 7th And Major 7th Chords

About Dominant 7th And Major 7th Chords:

Dominant 7th and major 7th chords are used extensively in Western music, and both chord types have a specific tonality, or sound. You don't have to understand the theory behind what makes a chord a dominant 7th or a major 7th chord to be able to hear and recognise the difference in sound, however the theory is very useful.

Before the theory, the sound! This is subjective, but typically dominant 7th chords sound as though they are unresolved and ‘want to go somewhere’ - almost like the second to last chord in a song - whereas major 7th chords sound more rested - like the ending chord of a jazz ballad. These different emotional feelings, and moving from one to another, help music tell stories.

More on the structure of Dominant 7th and Major 7th Chords

The simplest form of a chord is a 3 note triad, for example c major - notated as just a capital ‘C’ - which uses the notes c, e and g. The note ‘c’ is the root note of the chord, and both ‘e’ and ‘g’ are specific intervals (distances measured in semitones) above the root note ‘c’. In fact 2 notes together are not called a chord, they’re simply an ‘interval’. There are many different intervals each with different names. In our C chord the note ‘e’ is an interval of a ‘major third’ (4 semitones) above ‘c’, and the note ‘g’ is an interval of a ‘perfect fifth’ (7 semitones) above ‘c’. The note ‘g’ here is also a ‘minor 3rd’ (3 semitones) above the note ‘e’.

Intervals can be a lot to take in and they’re absolutely something worth understanding. You don’t necessarily need to know the names for them all, but you should at least understand the concept and be familiar with terms like ‘thirds’, ‘fifths’ and ‘sevenths’ - which brings us neatly back to our 7th chords.

A ‘dominant 7th’ chord has the three notes of the triad followed by the note which is an interval of a ‘minor 7th’ (10 semitones) above the root note - if you don’t want to count up 10 semitones from the root, just take the octave and go down 2 semitones.

So the chord of C (c,e,g) can be made into a C7 (C Dominant 7th) by adding the note 10 semitones above c, which is ‘b♭’. Therefore C7 (C Dominant 7th) contains the notes c, e, g and b♭.

A ‘major 7th’ chord is made in much the same way, except this time we add the note which is an interval of a ‘major 7th’ (11 semitones) above the root note - or again if you don’t want to count 11 semitones up from the root, take the root note’s octave and count 1 semitone down.

So C can become CMaj7 (C Major 7th) by adding the note 11 semitones above ‘c’, which is ‘b’. Therefore CMaj7 contains the notes c, e, g and b. Any of our standard minor or major triads can be made into a dominant 7th or a major 7th chord with the same logic of simply adding the note either a minor 7th interval above the root note (makes a dominant 7th chord) or the note a major 7th above the root note (makes a major 7th chord). Dominant 7th chords are written simply with the addition of a ‘7’, and major 7th chords are written by adding ‘Maj7’ to the chord name.

Examples of major chords in their dominant 7th or major 7th variations could be:
F7 (F dominant 7th), or FMaj7 (F major 7th)
G7 (G dominant 7th), GMaj7 (G major 7th)
C#7 (C# dominant 7th), C#Maj7 (C# major 7th) …

And examples of minor chords in their dominant 7th or major 7th variations could be:

Fm7, FmMaj7
Bbm7, BbmMaj7
Am7, AmMaj7

Sometimes the ‘Maj7’ section may be written in brackets, ie ‘Am(Maj7).

Further notes:

*There are 7 diatonic triads in each major scale, and each of these can have a single 7th variation, but not two - therefore there are also 7 diatonic 7th chords for each major scale. The reason there can only be one variation is that when we are restricted to only the 7 different notes of a single major scale, for any given root note only either the note an interval of a minor 7th or a major 7th above will be a note from the scale. A working example of this is:

Ie in D Major, the ii chord is Em (E minor, e, g, b), seven notes in the scale of D Major above the root note ‘e’ is ‘d’, so we get the notes ‘e’, ‘g’, ‘b’ and ‘d’. The note ‘d’ is 10 semitones above the root note ‘e’ which is an interval of a minor 7th above the root, which corresponds to a dominant 7th chord, so the ii chord in the key of D Major in it’s 7th variation is Em7. It couldn’t be Em(Maj7) because the note a major 7th (11 semitones) above the root note of ‘e’ would be ‘e♭’, which isn’t a note in the scale of D Major and as such can’t be used for a diatonic chord.

*Minor chords with major 7th notes on top (known as minor major 7ths, ie Am(Maj7)) are rare in Western music because they aren’t diatonic to any of the major scales, and it’s these major scales that form the theoretical foundation to most Western music. That is to say if we stick to making chords restricted to only the notes of a single major scale, ie C Major, none of the 7th chords produced will be minor chords with major 7th notes.
